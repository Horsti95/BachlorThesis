This chapter provides the theoretical foundation and contextual background necessary to understand and evaluate the fingerprint-based LOSO caching framework. The chapter follows a bottom-up progression from the data domain to the computational solution. Sections~\ref{sec:sleep_medicine} and \ref{sec:eeg_processing} introduce the sleep medicine domain and EEG signal processing concepts that define the validation scenario. Section~\ref{sec:sleep_classification} surveys sleep stage classification methods and positions this thesis within that literature. Section~\ref{sec:mlresearch_efficiency} covers ML research methodology and computational efficiency challenges. Finally, Section~\ref{sec:fingerprinting} introduces the configuration fingerprinting and caching technologies the framework builds upon.

\section{Sleep Medicine Fundamentals}
\label{sec:sleep_medicine}
% ============================================================================

The caching framework is domain-agnostic, but its validation in this thesis
is grounded in a specific application: automatic sleep stage classification
from EEG signals. The following sections introduce the sleep medicine domain
and EEG signal processing concepts that define the data, labels, and
feature space used in the experiments.

\subsection{Sleep Stages and Their Characteristics}

The American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM) defines five sleep stages,
scored in 30-second epochs from polysomnographic recordings~\cite{berry2017aasm}.
These stage labels constitute the classification targets ($y$-variables) in this
thesis:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Wake (W):} Low-amplitude, high-frequency EEG; alpha waves
      (8--13~Hz) predominate during relaxed wakefulness.

\item \textbf{N1 (Light Sleep):} mixed-frequency, low-amplitude EEG that is 
the most difficult stage to classify automatically. The difficulty has two 
compounding sources: N1 epochs are spectrally similar to both adjacent stages 
(Wake and N2), so even human scorers disagree on N1 boundaries~\cite{rosenberg2013aasm}; 
and N1 is severely underrepresented in real datasets (3.7\% of all epochs in BOAS, 
see Section~\ref{sec:data}), giving classifiers little signal to learn the stage's transitional pattern.

\item \textbf{N2 (Sleep):} Comprises 45–55\% of total sleep time and is the 
dominant class in BOAS at 60.3\% of all epochs (see Section~\ref{sec:data}). 
N2 is defined by sleep spindles (Sigma band, see Table~\ref{tab:eeg_bands}) 
and K-complexes; the abundance of training data and distinctive spectral markers 
make N2 the easiest stage to classify

\item \textbf{N3 (Deep Sleep):} Dominated by high-amplitude delta waves (0.5--4 Hz, $\geq 20\%$ of epoch)
and most prevalent in the first third of the night. The strong delta signature 
gives N3 a clear spectral fingerprint, but its absence in lighter-sleep periods 
means a per-subject classifier sees N3 only intermittently.

\item \textbf{REM:} low-amplitude, mixed-frequency EEG that is spectrally 
similar to wakefulness. Polysomnography distinguishes REM from Wake using 
two non-EEG channels, electrooculography (rapid eye movements) and 
electromyography (skeletal muscle atonia)~\cite{berry2017aasm}, but the 
EEG-only feature pipeline used in this thesis does not include EOG or EMG. 
The classifier therefore identifies REM from EEG alone, relying on subtler 
spectral and inter-channel features rather than the canonical eye-movement / 
muscle-tone markers. This adds intrinsic difficulty to REM classification 
beyond what AASM scoring tools experience.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{EEG Acquisition and the Need for Automation}

Electrodes are positioned according to the international 10–20 
standard~\cite{niedermeyer2011}, which uses a two-character label 
per electrode: the letter denotes the cortical region (F frontal, 
C central, O occipital) and the digit denotes the hemisphere (odd numbers 
left, even numbers right). The six channels used in this thesis, 
F3, F4, C3, C4, O1, O2, give bilateral coverage of frontal, sensorimotor, 
and visual cortices. In the caching framework, the channel list and the 
30-second epoch length are immutable preprocessing parameters and enter 
the SHA-256 fingerprint as such.

Manual sleep scoring by certified technologists requires approximately
1.5--2 hours per recording. For large cohorts such as the BOAS dataset
(128 subjects), manual annotation becomes prohibitively expensive,
motivating automated classification. Inter-rater agreement among human
experts is typically 80--90\%, providing a natural performance ceiling
for automated systems.

\section{EEG Signal Processing Basics}
\label{sec:eeg_processing}
% ============================================================================

EEG signals are non-stationary time series whose classification relies on
identifying characteristic oscillatory patterns in specific frequency
bands~\cite{niedermeyer2011}. These frequency bands form the basis of the
feature extraction pipeline and, consequently, a core parameter in the
cache fingerprint.

\subsection{Frequency Bands}

In this work, EEG signals are decomposed into six frequency bands.
Table~\ref{tab:eeg_bands} lists the ranges as implemented in the feature
extraction pipeline. Two boundaries deviate from the conventional literature
values~\cite{niedermeyer2011}: Beta starts at 16~Hz rather than the
conventional 13~Hz to avoid overlap with the Sigma band (12--16~Hz), and
Gamma is capped at 40~Hz to match the bandpass filter cutoff rather than
the commonly cited 45~Hz upper limit.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption[EEG frequency bands as used in this thesis]{EEG frequency bands
as implemented in this thesis and their sleep-stage associations.
Beta and Gamma boundaries differ slightly from conventional ranges
(see text).}
\label{tab:eeg_bands}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{Band} & \textbf{Range (Hz)} & \textbf{Sleep Stage Association} \\
\midrule
Delta  & 0.5--4.0   & Predominant in N3 (deep sleep); indicates sleep pressure \\
Theta    & 4.0--8.0   & Associated with N1 and REM; drowsiness marker \\
Alpha    & 8.0--13.0  & Relaxed wakefulness; key marker for Stage W \\
Sigma    & 12.0--16.0 & Sleep spindles; definitive marker for N2 sleep \\
Beta     & 16.0--30.0 & Active wakefulness; power decreases during NREM \\
Gamma    & 30.0--40.0 & High-frequency processing; typically minimal in sleep \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Power spectral density (PSD) in these bands, computed via Welch's method, forms the core of the frequency-domain feature extraction pipeline. By quantifying the relative power in these specific ranges, the XGBoost and Random Forest models can distinguish between the synchronized, high-amplitude waves of deep sleep and the desynchronized, low-amplitude patterns of wakefulness.

\subsection{Standard Preprocessing Pipeline}

Raw EEG signals contain several routine sources of contamination that are
addressed before feature extraction: low-frequency baseline drift and DC
offset (removed with a high-pass filter, typically $\geq 0.5$~Hz),
high-frequency noise (low-pass filter, typically $\leq 40$~Hz for sleep
staging), and 50/60~Hz power-line interference (notch filter). Recordings
are then downsampled to the minimum rate that preserves sleep-relevant
frequencies, and epochs contaminated by motion artefacts or amplitude
extremes may be rejected depending on dataset quality. The concrete filter
parameters, rejection thresholds, and downsampling rate used in this thesis
are reported with the rest of the preprocessing pipeline in
Section~\ref{sec:preprocessing}.

\section{Sleep Stage Classification Methods}
\label{sec:sleep_classification}
% ============================================================================

Sleep stage classification is a well-studied problem in biomedical signal
processing, with methods broadly grouped into feature-based and deep learning
approaches.

\subsection{Feature-Based Approaches}

Feature-based sleep staging extracts domain-informed descriptors from EEG
signals---time-domain statistics, frequency-domain band powers, and signal
complexity measures---and feeds them to classical ML
classifiers. Satapathy et al.\ (2024) demonstrate that multi-modality feature
sets with ensemble classifiers achieve competitive performance, particularly
in cross-subject settings where training data per subject is
limited~\cite{satapathy2024multimodal}.

This thesis follows the feature-based paradigm: 149 handcrafted features per
30-second epoch across 6 EEG channels, classified using XGBoost and Random
Forest. For reference, Bakian Dogaheh and Moradi (2023) report 81.3\%
accuracy and Kappa 0.74 using XGBoost on 5-class AASM staging from a
comparable EEG feature set~\cite{bakian2023xgboost}, providing a benchmark
against which the 85.5\% LOSO accuracy achieved in this thesis can be
situated. Feature-based models have the additional advantage of compact
serialized representations well-suited to fingerprint-based caching.

\subsection{Deep Learning Approaches}

Deep learning methods learn representations directly from raw EEG signals.
Perslev et al.\ (2021) present U-Sleep, achieving robust cross-hardware
staging~\cite{perslev2021usleep}. Siddiqui et al.\ (2025) confirm that deep
models require substantially more computational resources than traditional
ensembles while ensemble classifiers remain highly competitive on
standard benchmark datasets~\cite{siddiqui2025}. Deep learning workflows
address the reuse problem through \textit{checkpointing}---saving intermediate
training state to resume interrupted optimisation---rather than fold-level
memoisation. Checkpointing targets a different inefficiency and does not
prevent redundant retraining when only configuration parameters change.
This thesis addresses the complementary gap: fold-level memoisation for
feature-based models, where serialised representations range from
approximately 1--2~MB per fold (XGBoost) to 120--180~MB per fold
(Random Forest), and per-fold storage is tractable on a single workstation.

\section{Machine Learning Ensemble Methods}
\label{sec:ensemble_methods}
% ============================================================================
Ensemble methods combine multiple learners to produce a stronger predictor
than any individual model~\cite{hastie2009elements}. They are particularly
effective for tabular classification tasks, which motivates their use in
this thesis. Two ensemble strategies differ fundamentally in how they
aggregate learners: \textit{bagging} trains learners independently in
parallel (Random Forest), while \textit{boosting} trains them sequentially,
each correcting the errors of the previous (XGBoost). This section covers
both families.

\subsection{Decision Trees}

A decision tree recursively partitions the feature space by selecting
feature thresholds that maximise class separation (measured by Gini
impurity or information gain). Individual trees are fast to train but
prone to overfitting. Ensemble methods address this by combining many
trees trained under different conditions.

\subsection{Bagging: Random Forest}

Random Forest~\cite{breiman2001randomforest} fits an ensemble of decision trees in 
which each tree sees a bootstrap-resampled training set and, at every split, 
considers only a randomly drawn subset of the candidate features. Aggregating 
over many such weakly correlated trees by majority vote stabilises the prediction 
surface; the variance reduction this provides is the main reason ensemble methods 
outperform a single deep tree on noisy, high-dimensional inputs like EEG features.

Random Forest typically achieves strong out-of-the-box performance on
tabular data but produces large serialized model files (600--1{,}544~MB
per fold for 149 features and 127 training subjects). This storage
overhead is a key factor in its caching viability, as discussed in
Chapter~4.

\subsection{Boosting: XGBoost}

XGBoost (Extreme Gradient Boosting)~\cite{chen2016xgboost} extends
gradient boosting with
regularisation terms, sparsity-aware splitting, and cache-efficient tree
construction. Unlike Random Forest, boosting models use shallow trees
(depth 3--6) with aggressive regularisation, producing compact serialized
representations of 1--2~MB per fold---making them highly cache-viable.

\subsection{Why These Models for EEG Feature Classification}

Random Forest and XGBoost are selected for this thesis because they
represent two contrasting ensemble strategies (bagging vs.\ boosting)
with fundamentally different serialization characteristics. This contrast
directly serves the caching evaluation: XGBoost produces compact models
that are fast to cache and load, while Random Forest produces large models
that stress-test the caching framework's viability boundaries.
Additionally, both models handle the 149-dimensional feature space
effectively and provide interpretable feature importance scores for
post-hoc analysis.

\section{Cross-Validation in Subject-Independent ML}
\label{sec:loso_background}
% ============================================================================

EEG signals exhibit high inter-subject variability: brain activity patterns
differ substantially between individuals due to anatomical differences,
electrode placement tolerances, and inter-individual variation in
neurophysiology~\cite{huang2023intersubject}. This variability means that
a model trained on data from subjects $S_1, \ldots, S_{N-1}$ and evaluated
on a held-in portion of those same subjects' data will overestimate real-world
performance on a new, unseen subject. Standard $k$-fold cross-validation does
not account for this: train and test folds typically contain epochs from the
same subjects, producing accuracy estimates that are misleadingly optimistic
for subject-independent deployment.

Leave-One-Subject-Out (LOSO) cross-validation addresses this by training
on all subjects except one and evaluating on the excluded subject, cycling
through every subject as the test case. Kunjan et al.\ demonstrate directly
that for scalp-recorded EEG classification, $k$-fold results are misleading
for unseen-subject performance, and conclude that LOSO evaluation is
necessary for any EEG-based machine learning study~\cite{kunjan2021loso}.

The computational cost is proportional to the cohort size: with $N = 128$
subjects, a single experimental configuration requires 128 separate model
training runs. Each run produces a distinct trained model valid only for
evaluating its specific held-out subject,   a model trained with subject
$S_i$ excluded cannot be reused to evaluate subject $S_j$, because the
training population differs. This fold-specificity is what makes LOSO the
primary driver of the caching framework: without persistent, fold-aware
storage of trained models, every configuration change triggers 128
redundant retraining operations regardless of how little the configuration
actually changed.

\section{Machine Learning Research Methodology and Computational Efficiency}
\label{sec:mlresearch_efficiency}
\label{sec:mlresearch}
\label{sec:comp_efficiency}

ML research is iterative by construction. A typical project cycles through preprocessing tweaks, feature-set redefinitions, hyperparameter sweeps, and architecture variants, with each cycle re-running the same long computations against slightly different inputs. The cumulative cost, in compute hours, energy, and developer time, is dominated not by any single training run but by the dozens of repetitions a project accumulates while converging on a workable design \cite{sculley2015hidden}. Standardised lifecycle models such as CRISP-DM~\cite{crispdm2000} formalise this iteration as an explicit cycle that returns repeatedly to the modelling phase. For deployed ML systems, CRISP-ML(Q)~\cite{studer2021crispmlq} extends this lifecycle perspective with explicit quality assurance, monitoring, and maintenance tasks to address risks such as model degradation, bias, and lack of reproducibility. Each return to the modelling phase triggers full retraining unless intermediate artefacts are persisted across cycles, which is exactly the gap the fingerprint-based framework in this thesis fills, by memoising trained models under a cryptographic key so that re-running the modelling phase becomes a cache lookup rather than a recomputation.

Tools such as MLflow~\cite{zaharia2018mlflow} and MLXP~\cite{arbel2024mlxp} support experiment tracking by recording parameters, metrics, implementation versions, and artefacts for each run, enabling comparison and reproducibility. These tools were designed for single train/test splits and tabular grid searches. In Leave-One-Subject-Out cross-validation with 128 subjects, a single parameter change invalidates 128 individually trained models rather than one—making the absence of fold-aware caching not merely inconvenient but a potential source of silent scientific errors.

Reproducibility remains a pervasive challenge in ML research~\cite{semmelrock2025reproducibility}. Kreuzberger et al.\ define reproducibility as the ability to obtain identical results under identical conditions (Principle P3) and emphasise metadata tracking for model lineage (Principle P7)~\cite{kreuzberger2023mlops}. Achieving P3 requires controlling three interdependent components: input data, tracked parameters, and the execution environment. Challenges arise from:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Lack of Records and Hyperparameter Inconsistency:} Unrecorded decisions or parameter changes hinder replication.
    \item \textbf{Data and Environment Volatility:} Dataset alterations, framework updates, and inconsistent compute environments cause divergent outcomes. Rzig et al.\ find that 61.8\% of CI/CD pipeline modifications in ML projects address build policies and dependency management rather than model logic~\cite{rzig2024empirical}.
    \item \textbf{Inherent Randomness:} Stochastic initialisation and hardware-level non-determinism (e.g., NVIDIA cuDNN atomic operations~\cite{nvidia2024cudnn}) affect deep learning models. The classical ensemble models used in this thesis (sklearn, XGBoost, fixed \texttt{random\_state=42}) are deterministic by construction and unaffected by these concerns.
\end{itemize}

The increasing complexity and scale of Machine Learning models have substantially raised computational demands. Training models, tuning hyperparameters, and running repeated experiments consume vast compute, memory, and time resources. Several factors drive this resource consumption:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Large Datasets:} Massive datasets require intensive I/O and memory usage.
    \item \textbf{Complex Models:} Both deep neural networks and ensemble methods (e.g., gradient boosting with thousands of trees, large random forests) require substantial computational resources for training and inference.
    \item \textbf{Iterative Experimentation:} Frequent experiments often repeat computations unnecessarily when modifying a subset of parameters while keeping others constant.
    \item \textbf{Hyperparameter Optimization:} Searching large hyperparameter spaces is inherently compute-intensive.
    \item \textbf{Rigorous Validation:} Cross-validation strategies (e.g., k-fold, leave-one-out) multiply training costs by the number of folds.
\end{itemize}

While caching can help, traditional research in cache management—such as the ML-aided hardware strategies surveyed by Krishna (2025)—focuses primarily on optimizing low-level latency and hardware replacement policies~\cite{krishna2025cache}. These systems are designed to predict microsecond-level access patterns rather than addressing the macro-level requirement of matching high-level experiment configurations to saved artefacts. For EEG research, such low-level caching does not prevent the redundant recomputation of 128-fold cross-validation when hyperparameters change.

Caching shifts the bottleneck from computation time to storage space, a trade-off whose viability depends on the serialised model size, the expected number of re-runs, and available storage capacity. For compact models re-run frequently, caching is clearly beneficial; for very large models run only once, the storage overhead may outweigh the savings. This thesis addresses this trade-off explicitly through a model viability analysis in Chapter~\ref{chap:methodology}.

\section{Technology: Configuration Fingerprinting and Caching}
\label{sec:fingerprinting}
% ============================================================================

Efficient ML experimentation depends on configuration management and intelligent
result reuse. This section reviews technologies directly relevant to the
fingerprint-based LOSO caching approach developed in this thesis.

As established in Section~\ref{sec:loso_background}, LOSO cross-validation
is necessary for EEG-based ML to avoid inflated generalisation estimates.
Its computational cost,   128 training runs per configuration,   makes it
the primary driver of the caching framework: 2{,}304 required training
operations provide a high-volume workload where deterministic fingerprinting
delivers maximum efficiency gains.

\subsection{Configuration Fingerprinting}

Configuration fingerprinting uses cryptographic hash functions (e.g., SHA-256) 
to create unique identifiers for experiment configurations. By hashing a 
canonical representation of all parameters, these fingerprints enable 
deterministic identification of identical configurations and automatic 
cache invalidation when parameters change.

Modern MLOps tools employ variants of this approach:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{DVC} logs pipeline signatures combining dependency contents, 
          parameters, and commands~\cite{dvc_pipelines}.
    \item \textbf{MLflow} tracks experiment metadata to enable versioning and 
          visual comparison~\cite{zaharia2018mlflow}.
    \item \textbf{MLXP} enforces reproducibility by linking Hydra-based 
          configurations with specific Git commit hashes~\cite{arbel2024mlxp}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Hash-Based Caching in MLOps}

A terminological note is warranted before proceeding. In traditional computer
science, a \textit{cache} is temporary and subject to eviction (e.g., CPU
L1/L2 caches, browser caches). In the context of MLOps and this thesis,
\textit{caching} refers to the persistent memoisation of computational
artefacts to disk---a permanent store keyed by a cryptographic fingerprint
of the configuration that produced them. These artefacts are not evicted;
they persist until explicitly deleted. The distinction within this thesis is:
the Fingerprint-based Model Cache (Tier~2) behaves as a true cache in that
a fingerprint mismatch bypasses the stored result; the Persistent Feature
Store (Tier~1) is a permanent store that is never invalidated by design.
What elevates any persistent memoisation above cold file storage is the
automated check-and-bypass protocol: the pipeline queries for a key before
computing, and loads the stored result if found rather than recomputing.

MLOps pipeline caching implements content-addressable storage: when pipeline
inputs and tracked parameters match previous runs (verified through hash comparison),
cached outputs are reused~\cite{dvc_pipelines}. DVC's run cache,
for example, combines dependency hashes with parameter configurations to
create cache keys. When a match is found, execution is skipped and previous
results are restored.

This approach provides reproducibility: identical configurations produce
identical cache keys, ensuring cached results correspond to recorded
parameters~\cite{schubotz2022caching}.

In the context of Google's MLOps maturity model~\cite{googlemlops2023}, automated pipeline execution---including artefact reuse---is a key component for progressing from Level~0 (manual processes) to Level~1 (automated training pipelines). It serves not only as an efficiency mechanism but as the technical implementation of \textit{traceability}: only through unique configuration fingerprints can the exact data and parameter versions that produced a specific artefact be identified with certainty. Fingerprinting addresses the controllable sources of irreproducibility---seeds, hyperparameters, data splits, implementation versions---while for the CPU-based sklearn and XGBoost models used in this thesis (fixed \texttt{random\_state=42}), deterministic outputs are guaranteed by construction.
