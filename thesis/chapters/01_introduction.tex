\chapter{Introduction}


\section{Introduction and Motivation}

This thesis grew out of a practical problem encountered during a sleep lab project
in a previous university course. The project involved EEG-based sleep stage
classification --- training machine learning models to automatically label
30-second sleep epochs as Wake, N1, N2, N3, or REM. The classification task itself
worked. What did not work was the iteration speed.

Leave-One-Subject-Out (LOSO) cross-validation, the standard evaluation protocol
for subject-independent sleep staging, requires training one model per subject.
With 128 subjects, every parameter change --- adjusting a correlation threshold,
trying a different feature count, switching from XGBoost to Random Forest ---
triggered a full retraining of 128 models. A single Random Forest run across
128 subjects took over eight hours. Exploring 9 configurations meant days of
wall-clock time for a parameter sweep that should take minutes.

The question that arose was not ``how do we improve the classifier?'' but ``why
are we retraining models that haven't changed?'' Most models in a LOSO sweep
remain valid when only a handful of parameters are modified. The computational
waste was not a classification problem --- it was a caching problem.

This observation motivated a shift in focus: from sleep classification as the goal
to caching optimization as the contribution. The sleep staging task became the
validation vehicle for a more general framework addressing redundant computation
in iterative ML experimentation.

The broader landscape reinforces why this matters. The field of Artificial
Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning (ML) is experiencing unprecedented growth,
with an explosive increase in both academic publications and industrial
applications~\cite{AIIndex2023}. According to the Stanford AI Index Report 2023,
AI-related publications increased approximately 2.3 times since 2015, and
collaborations between educational institutions and industry grew 4.2 times from
2010 to 2021. As ML systems grow more complex, the cost of redundant computation
scales accordingly. MLOps (Machine Learning Operations) pipelines aim to
standardize development, boost productivity, and enable reproducible
experimentation~\cite{kreuzberger2023mlops} --- but caching for cross-validation
workflows remains an unaddressed gap.

\section{Research Problem}

Despite rapid advancements in ML, iterative experimentation workflows face
significant computational inefficiencies, particularly in cross-validation
scenarios. Leave-One-Subject-Out (LOSO) cross-validation, commonly used to
evaluate model generalisation across individuals, requires training \textit{N}
models for \textit{N} subjects. In the experiments conducted for this thesis,
a single 9-configuration XGBoost sweep across 128 subjects required
approximately 12{,}500 seconds ($\approx$3.5 hours) from cold start. Random
Forest was substantially worse: a single 9-configuration sweep required over
77{,}000 seconds ($\approx$21 hours). A full 18-configuration experiment
therefore spans from several hours (XGBoost) to multiple days (Random Forest)
per complete cold run. When researchers iterate on hyperparameters or feature
selection strategies, this entire process must be repeated, even when most
trained models remain valid under the new configuration.

This thesis addresses three interconnected challenges in managing LOSO
experimentation workflows:

\begin{itemize}

\item \textbf{Redundant Computation Across Experimental Iterations:}
ML experimentation requires repeatedly modifying specific components
(hyperparameters, feature subsets, model architectures) while keeping others
constant. Existing MLOps tools such as DVC~\cite{dvc2022inproceedings} and
ZenML provide hierarchical pipeline caching, where downstream changes do not
invalidate upstream artifacts. However, adapting these tools to LOSO workflows
requires explicit parameterisation of the held-out subject and careful cache
key design, a process that is neither standardised nor validated in existing
literature. Without a validated framework, researchers face a choice between
no caching (prohibitive recomputation costs) or manual cache management
(error-prone and difficult to reproduce).

\item \textbf{Ensuring Cache Validity Across Cross-Validation Folds:}
In LOSO cross-validation, each fold's trained model is valid only for
evaluating its specific held-out subject. A model trained with subject $S_i$
held out produces scientifically invalid predictions when applied to evaluate
subject $S_j$ held out --- not due to data leakage (the train/test split is
maintained) but due to applying cached results to an incorrect experimental
condition.

While general-purpose caching systems like DVC can be configured to handle this
by parameterising the held-out subject, there is no validated framework
demonstrating best practices for LOSO cache key design. Without explicit
guidance, researchers risk either overly conservative cache invalidation
(recomputing valid results) or incorrect parameterisation (potential cache
validity violations). This thesis established a validated approach where
held-out subject identifiers are systematically included in fingerprint-based
cache keys.

\item \textbf{Unified Fingerprinting for Reproducibility and Cache Validity:}
Existing MLOps tools separate experiment tracking (MLflow, W\&B) from
pipeline caching (DVC, ZenML), requiring researchers to manually coordinate
logged configurations with cached artifacts~\cite{zaharia2018mlflow,%
wandb2024docs,dvc2022inproceedings}. This separation becomes particularly
challenging in LOSO scenarios where fold identity must be consistently tracked
across both systems. This thesis implemented cryptographic fingerprinting
(SHA-256 of canonical JSON configurations) as a unified solution: a single
fingerprint simultaneously ensures cache validity, reproducibility, and
experiment identification. The approach was validated by demonstrating
deterministic fingerprint generation across 18 experimental configurations
and 128 cross-validation folds, with zero fingerprint collisions and
complete result reproducibility.

\end{itemize}

This thesis addresses these challenges through a fingerprint-based caching
framework for LOSO cross-validation experiments. The framework uses SHA-256
hashing of canonical JSON configuration representations to generate deterministic
cache keys, with held-out subject identifiers included to ensure cache validity
across folds. The approach was validated through a sleep stage classification
case study using 128 subjects from the BOAS (Bitbrain Open Access Sleep)
dataset.

\section{Research Questions}

\textbf{Main Research Question:}

How can fingerprint-based caching reduce computational costs in
Leave-One-Subject-Out cross-validation while ensuring reproducibility
and preventing data leakage?

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{RQ1 (Efficiency):} What time savings and cache hit rates were
achieved through fingerprint-based caching of extracted features and trained
models in LOSO cross-validation?
\item \textbf{RQ2 (Reproducibility):} How does SHA-256 configuration
fingerprinting ensure consistent and reproducible experiment results across
multiple runs?
\item \textbf{RQ3 (Scalability):} How does caching performance scale with
dataset size, from 10 subjects to the full 128-subject dataset?
\item \textbf{RQ4 (Trade-offs):} What are the storage costs versus
computational savings of the caching approach, and under what conditions
does caching remain viable?
\end{itemize}


\section{Research Approach}

This thesis followed a quantitative experimental methodology. The fingerprint-based
caching framework was implemented, deployed on a real EEG sleep staging pipeline,
and evaluated through controlled benchmarks. The research approach comprised three
phases:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Framework Implementation:} Design and implement the SHA-256
fingerprinting system, feature cache, and LOSO model cache. Validate
correctness through unit tests and cache invalidation tests.
\item \textbf{Benchmark Experiments:} Execute the full 18-configuration
experiment (2 models $\times$ 3 correlation thresholds $\times$ 3 feature
counts, 128-fold LOSO each) under cold-start and warm-cache conditions.
Measure speedup, storage, and accuracy for each configuration.
\item \textbf{Extended Validation:} Conduct viability analysis across 15
model types, scaling analysis from 10 to 128 subjects, and feature
selection benchmarks (ANOVA vs.\ Mutual Information).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Research Method and Approach}

The experimental evaluation combined:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Performance Measurement:} Systematic collection of timing
metrics, cache hit rates, and storage utilisation across multiple
experimental scenarios.
\item \textbf{Comparative Analysis:} Evaluation of caching efficiency across
different conditions (cold start vs.\ cached runs, identical vs.\ modified
configurations).
\item \textbf{Reproducibility Validation:} Verification that identical
fingerprints produce identical results across multiple runs.
\end{itemize}

The approach centres on two orthogonal evaluation dimensions:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{WHY to cache:} Optimisation goals including latency reduction,
throughput improvement, and cumulative time savings across iterative experiments.
\item \textbf{WHERE to cache:} The framework implements hierarchical caching
at two levels. Feature-level caching stores 149 extracted features per epoch,
computed once per subject (implemented, achieving 227$\times$ speedup).
LOSO model caching stores trained models per cross-validation fold, with the
held-out subject identifier encoded in the cache key to prevent data leakage
(implemented and validated across all 18 configurations).
\end{itemize}

\section{Structure of the Thesis}
This thesis is structured as follows:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Chapter 1: Introduction} presents the motivation rooted in
practical LOSO runtime problems, the research problem, research questions,
and research approach.

\item \textbf{Chapter 2: Background} covers ML research methodology,
computational efficiency challenges, sleep medicine fundamentals, EEG signal
processing basics, ML ensemble methods, and configuration fingerprinting
and caching technologies.

\item \textbf{Chapter 3: Related Works} reviews existing MLOps tools
(MLflow, DVC, ZenML), sleep stage classification methods grouped by technique,
and identifies the gap in LOSO-aware caching that this work addresses.

\item \textbf{Chapter 4: Methodology and Results} details the fingerprint-based
caching framework, the BOAS dataset, preprocessing pipeline, extraction of
149 EEG features per epoch, the LOSO cross-validation experimental design,
and the full experimental results including cache speedups, classification
performance, model viability analysis, and discussion.

\item \textbf{Chapter 5: Summary and Future Work} summarises the contributions
of the fingerprint-based caching framework and discusses extensions including
two-tier RAM+Disk caching and cross-experiment cache reuse.
\end{itemize}
