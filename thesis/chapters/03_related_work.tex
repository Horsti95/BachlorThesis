\chapter{Related Works}

This chapter reviews existing solutions for ML experiment caching and sleep
stage classification, and identifies the specific gap this thesis addresses:
the lack of validated best practices for caching in Leave-One-Subject-Out
(LOSO) cross-validation scenarios.

% ============================================================================
\section{Academic Research on ML Caching}
% ============================================================================

Research at the intersection of caching and machine learning divides into two
complementary directions: \textit{ML-for-System-Optimisation}, where learned
models predict what to cache in hardware or CDN systems, and
\textit{Caching-for-ML-Workflows}, where deterministic artefact stores
accelerate scientific experiments. This thesis belongs firmly to the second
category.

Krishna~(2025) surveys the first direction, reviewing LSTM-based usage
prediction and reinforcement-learning replacement policies that improve hardware
cache throughput~\cite{krishna2025cache}. These approaches optimise
\textit{learned} decisions for hardware efficiency; they make no claim about
scientific reproducibility and are not designed to validate that a cache hit
corresponds to an identical experimental configuration.
Schubotz et al.\ (2022) operate in the second direction, demonstrating how
content-addressable storage can accelerate data-science experiments while
maintaining FAIR~(Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable)
principles~\cite{schubotz2022caching}. Their work establishes that
fingerprint-based artefact reuse is a sound engineering pattern for
reproducible research --- a foundation this thesis extends to the LOSO
cross-validation setting.

Two recent papers extend the scope of ML pipeline caching to AutoML and
hyperparameter optimisation. Hirzel et al.\ (2024) show how entire pipelines
can be trained and cross-validated on limited-memory hardware by decomposing
computations into monoid-structured task graphs that avoid redundant
recomputation of shared prefixes~\cite{hirzel2024pipelines}. While
memory-efficient and compatible with standard sklearn APIs, the approach
treats fold outputs as interchangeable: the task graph does not encode fold
identity into cache keys, so it cannot prevent cross-fold contamination in
LOSO settings where each fold corresponds to a structurally different training
set.
Essofi et al.\ (2024) address hyperparameter search cost with EEIPU, a
memoization-aware Bayesian optimisation algorithm that caches pipeline-stage
outputs to evaluate more configurations per GPU-day~\cite{essofi2024eeipu}.
EEIPU exploits the fact that shared pipeline prefixes produce identical
outputs across hyperparameter candidates. This assumption holds for i.i.d.\
splits but breaks in LOSO cross-validation, where the training prefix differs
by definition for each held-out subject.

Taken together, these papers confirm that memoization is a validated
acceleration technique for ML pipelines and hyperparameter search.
Neither work, however, provides a validated pattern for fold-aware caching in
LOSO scenarios. The challenge of encoding held-out subject identity into a
deterministic cache key --- and validating cache integrity against fold
assignments to prevent silent cross-subject contamination --- remains
unaddressed in the cited literature. This thesis fills that gap with a
reference implementation and empirical benchmarks on a 128-subject dataset.

\section{Commercial MLOps Tools}
% ============================================================================
A structural limitation of these platforms emerges from their design focus. As Kreuzberger et al.\ observe, workflow orchestration tools such as Airflow, Kubeflow, and AWS SageMaker are optimised for linear production pipelines~\cite{kreuzberger2023mlops}. They lack deterministic sub-task invalidation for nested experimental loops such as LOSO cross-validation, where 128 subjects $\times$ 18 configurations create 2{,}304 interdependent but individually cacheable training operations. Furthermore, their cost management principles focus on periodic retraining schedules rather than state-aware retraining, where computation is skipped entirely when the underlying configuration has not changed. The framework developed in this thesis fills this gap at the experimental stage by providing a pre-computation check that prevents redundant training before it begins.

Current MLOps platforms provide general experiment tracking and pipeline
caching but are agnostic to the internal structure of cross-validation. While these tools can be configured for LOSO via complex templating, they do not natively encode fold-identity into cache keys, leading to coarse-grained invalidation where changing a single parameter might trigger an entire 128-fold recomputation unnecessarily.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{MLflow:} Provides experiment tracking, model registry, and
      artifact logging~\cite{zaharia2018mlflow}. While pipeline caching is
      available through extension modules, standard MLflow workflows are
      not documented to encode fold assignments in cache keys, requiring
      custom implementation for LOSO scenarios.

\item \textbf{DVC:} Uses MD5 hashes of pipeline inputs and code to enable
      pipeline stage reuse~\cite{dvc_pipelines}. DVC's key covers code and
      data files but carries no fold-identity component. A cache hit therefore
      cannot distinguish a model trained on subjects 1--127 from one trained on
      subjects 2--128 --- the silent collision this thesis prevents by embedding
      the held-out subject identifier directly in the SHA-256 fingerprint.

\item \textbf{ZenML:} Implements step-level SHA-256 caching with automatic
      invalidation when inputs or code change. However, ZenML does not natively
      encode held-out subject identifiers: users must implement custom
      materializers to parameterise fold assignments, and the resulting keys
      provide no integrity guarantee that training and evaluation sets are
      correctly separated across cache hits.

\item \textbf{Neptune AI and Weights \& Biases:} While these platforms excel at 
      metric visualization and experiment logging~\cite{wandb2024docs}, 
      they are inherently subject to platform stability and vendor lock-in. 
      The discontinuation of Neptune AI services in early 2026~\cite{neptune2026shutdown} 
      vividly underscores the long-term risks of relying on proprietary, cloud-based 
      metadata stores for academic research. Furthermore, these tools primarily 
      function as observation layers; they lack integrated computational caching 
      mechanisms. This necessitates the development of the independent, 
      self-hosted framework for reproducible LOSO-fold management proposed 
      in this thesis.
\end{itemize}

\section{Research Gap Analysis}
% ============================================================================
Despite the widespread adoption of CRISP-DM as the standard process model for data mining projects~\cite{schroer2021crispdmslr}, and the subsequent quality assurance extensions proposed by CRISP-ML(Q)~\cite{studer2021crispmlq}, these frameworks remain at a procedural level---they prescribe \textit{what} should be done but do not enforce \textit{how} reproducibility is achieved technically. The framework developed in this thesis addresses this gap by enforcing reproducibility not as a guideline but through strict configuration fingerprinting and caching, eliminating the manual error sources identified as a key barrier to ML maturity~\cite{googlemlops2023}.

The core gap is the lack of validated, documented patterns for
implementing fold-aware caching in cross-validation workflows. While tools
like DVC and ZenML provide general pipeline caching infrastructure, adapting
them for LOSO requires custom logic that:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Encodes the held-out subject identifier in cache keys to prevent
      cross-fold contamination
\item Manages hierarchical dependencies between preprocessing, feature
      extraction, and model training stages
\item Validates cache hits against both parameter configurations and
      fold assignments
\end{enumerate}

These requirements are not addressed in standard documentation, and incorrect
implementation risks subtle bugs where models trained on Subject~A are
erroneously reused for evaluating Subject~B.

This thesis provides a reference implementation demonstrating these patterns
in a real LOSO scenario (128-fold cross-validation on the BOAS dataset), with
explicit measurement of cache performance (hit rates, time savings) and
validation of reproducibility. The contribution is not a new MLOps platform,
but a validated approach for extending existing fingerprinting
techniques to cross-validation contexts.

\section{Sleep Stage Classification Methods}
\label{sec:related_sleep}
% ============================================================================

Sleep stage classification is a well-studied problem in biomedical signal
processing, with methods broadly grouped into feature-based and deep learning
approaches.

\subsection{Feature-Based Approaches}

Feature-based sleep staging extracts domain-informed descriptors from EEG
signals---time-domain statistics, frequency-domain band powers, and signal
complexity measures---and feeds them to classical ML
classifiers. Satapathy et al.\ (2024) demonstrate that multi-modality feature
sets with ensemble classifiers achieve competitive performance, particularly
in cross-subject settings where training data per subject is
limited~\cite{satapathy2024multimodal}.

This thesis follows the feature-based paradigm: 149 handcrafted features per
30-second epoch across 6 EEG channels, classified using XGBoost and Random
Forest. Feature-based models have the advantage of compact serialized
representations well-suited to fingerprint-based caching.

\subsection{Deep Learning Approaches}

Deep learning methods learn representations directly from raw EEG signals.
Perslev et al.\ (2021) present U-Sleep, achieving robust cross-hardware
staging~\cite{perslev2021usleep}. Siddiqui et al.\ (2025) confirm that deep
models require substantially more computational resources than traditional
ensembles while ensemble classifiers remain highly competitive on
standard benchmark datasets~\cite{siddiqui2025}. Deep learning workflows
address the reuse problem through \textit{checkpointing}---saving intermediate
training state to resume interrupted optimisation---rather than fold-level
memoisation. Checkpointing targets a different inefficiency and does not
prevent redundant retraining when only configuration parameters change.
This thesis addresses the complementary gap: fold-level memoisation for
feature-based models, where serialised representations are compact
(1--185~MB per fold) and per-fold storage is tractable on a single
workstation.

\subsection{LOSO Evaluation in EEG Research}

LOSO cross-validation is preferred in EEG research because EEG signals
contain strong subject-specific signatures. LOSO ensures the model is
evaluated on subjects it has never seen, providing a conservative estimate
of generalisation. The computational cost scales directly with subject pool
size: 128 subjects $\times$ 18 configurations yields 2{,}304 training
operations, making caching essential for practical parameter search.

\section{Industrial Research and Applications}
\label{sec:related_industrial}
% ============================================================================

The gap between academic sleep staging research and industrial deployment
reflects broader challenges in production machine learning~\cite{kreuzberger2023mlops}.
Academic studies typically report results on controlled datasets under ideal
acquisition conditions, while commercial sleep monitoring must cope with
consumer-grade hardware, motion artefacts, and variable electrode contact
quality.

\textbf{Commercial Sleep Monitoring:}
Consumer wearables---smartwatches, fitness trackers, and optical heart rate
bands---incorporate simplified sleep staging primarily from accelerometer
and photoplethysmography signals rather than full EEG. This hardware
simplification substantially reduces staging accuracy, particularly for
discriminating N1 from N2 and for reliably identifying REM. The
industry-standard approach currently accepts this accuracy trade-off in
exchange for user convenience and mass-market scalability.
Medical-grade consumer EEG headbands represent an intermediate tier,
providing dry-electrode frontal EEG channels at a fraction of clinical
PSG complexity. Devices in this category have narrowed the accuracy gap
relative to clinical PSG, but introduce a different deployment challenge:
models trained on full clinical EEG (such as those in this thesis, using
6 channels at 256~Hz) must be retrained or adapted for the reduced channel
count and placement, requiring the exact kind of iterative experimentation
that fingerprint-based caching accelerates.

\textbf{Production ML Challenges:}
Deploying ML models in clinical and consumer contexts introduces requirements
rarely addressed in academic benchmarks: model versioning across firmware
updates, inference latency constraints for real-time feedback, regulatory
approval requiring full audit trails, and reproducibility across device
batches with sensor variability. As Kreuzberger et al.\ observe, fewer than
15\% of ML proof-of-concept projects successfully reach production
deployment~\cite{kreuzberger2023mlops}, with reproducibility and experiment
management identified as primary barriers.

\textbf{Research-to-Production Gap:}
The transition from research prototype to production deployment requires
systematic experiment management: the ability to reproduce any prior result
on demand, resume interrupted experiments, and compare model versions
without retraining from scratch. These requirements are precisely what
fingerprint-based caching provides. A researcher iterating a sleep staging
model toward clinical deployment---adjusting feature sets, testing on new
subject populations, validating on different hardware---benefits directly
from a framework that caches each validated configuration and prevents
re-running computations when only downstream parameters change. The
framework developed in this thesis addresses this gap at the experimental
stage, providing the infrastructure foundation that production deployment
requires.

\textbf{Configuration Debt and Technical Reproducibility:}
The systems consequences of undisciplined experiment management are
well-documented. Sculley et al.\ (2015) identify \textit{hidden technical
debt} in production ML systems, specifically \textit{configuration debt},
where undocumented parameter interactions and stale pipeline configurations
accumulate silently and degrade reproducibility over
time~\cite{sculley2015hidden}. Fingerprint-based caching directly addresses
this class of debt: because every cached artefact is keyed by a cryptographic
hash of its complete configuration, it is structurally impossible to serve a
stale result from a superseded parameter set. In regulated deployment
contexts---such as medical-device software subject to FDA or EU AI Act audit
requirements---this property provides a natural audit trail, linking every
stored model to the exact configuration that produced it.
