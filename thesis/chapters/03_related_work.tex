\chapter{Related Works}

This chapter surveys related work along two tracks that together motivate
and contextualise this thesis. The first track
(Sections~\ref{sec:related_academic}--\ref{sec:related_gap}) reviews
existing approaches to ML experiment caching---academic memoisation
research, commercial MLOps tools, and their shared limitation: none provides
validated, fold-aware caching for Leave-One-Subject-Out (LOSO)
cross-validation. The second track
(Sections~\ref{sec:related_sleep}--\ref{sec:related_industrial}) positions
the application domain: it surveys sleep stage classification methods to
establish where this thesis sits in that literature, and examines industrial
deployment challenges to show why reproducibility and configuration
management remain unsolved in practice. Together, the two tracks establish
both the methodological gap this thesis fills and the domain in which it is
validated.

% ============================================================================
\section{Academic Research on ML Caching}
\label{sec:related_academic}
% ============================================================================

Research at the intersection of caching and machine learning divides into two
complementary directions: ML-for-System-Optimisation, where learned
models predict what to cache in hardware or CDN systems, and
Caching-for-ML-Workflows, where deterministic artefact stores
accelerate scientific experiments. The framework developed in this thesis
operates within the second direction.

Krishna~(2025) surveys the first direction, reviewing LSTM-based usage
prediction and reinforcement-learning replacement policies that improve hardware
cache throughput~\cite{krishna2025cache}. These approaches optimise
\textit{learned} decisions for hardware efficiency; they make no claim about
scientific reproducibility and are not designed to validate that a cache hit
corresponds to an identical experimental configuration.
Schubotz et al.\ (2022) operate in the second direction, demonstrating how
content-addressable storage can accelerate data-science experiments while
maintaining FAIR~(Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable)
principles~\cite{schubotz2022caching}. Their work establishes that
fingerprint-based artefact reuse is a sound engineering pattern for
reproducible research --- a foundation this thesis extends to the LOSO
cross-validation setting.

Two recent papers extend ML pipeline caching to AutoML and hyperparameter
optimisation. Hirzel et al.\ (2024) decompose pipelines into task graphs so
that shared pipeline prefixes are computed once and reused within a single
training or cross-validation run, reducing redundant computation on
memory-constrained hardware~\cite{hirzel2024pipelines}. Their approach
targets waste \textit{within} a run; this thesis targets waste \textit{across}
repeated experimental iterations such as hyperparameter sweeps. While
memory-efficient and compatible with standard sklearn APIs, Hirzel et al.\
treat fold outputs as interchangeable: the task graph does not encode fold
identity into cache keys, so it cannot prevent cross-fold contamination in
LOSO settings where each fold corresponds to a structurally different training
set.
Essofi et al.\ (2024) address hyperparameter search cost with EEIPU, a
memoization-aware Bayesian optimisation algorithm that caches pipeline-stage
outputs to evaluate more configurations per GPU-day~\cite{essofi2024eeipu}.
EEIPU exploits the fact that shared pipeline prefixes produce identical
outputs across hyperparameter candidates. This assumption holds for i.i.d.\
splits but breaks in LOSO cross-validation, where the training prefix differs
by definition for each held-out subject.

Taken together, these papers confirm that memoization is a validated
acceleration technique for ML pipelines and hyperparameter search.
Neither work, however, provides a validated pattern for fold-aware caching in
LOSO scenarios. Adjacent caching paradigms---LLM inference caching
(KV-cache management~\cite{kwon2023pagedattention}), layer-skipping for deep
networks~\cite{balasubramanian2021accelerating}, and petabyte-scale HEP data
distribution~\cite{sim2023xcache}---address different problem structures and
make no claim about cross-validation fold integrity. The challenge of encoding
held-out subject identity into a deterministic cache key --- and validating
cache integrity against fold assignments to prevent silent cross-subject
contamination --- remains unaddressed in the cited literature. This thesis
fills that gap with a reference implementation and empirical benchmarks on a
128-subject dataset.

\section{Commercial MLOps Tools}
\label{sec:related_commercial}
% ============================================================================
Commercial MLOps platforms---MLflow, DVC, ZenML, and experiment-tracking tools
such as Weights \& Biases---provide general pipeline caching and experiment
management infrastructure. A structural limitation common to these tools
emerges from their design focus. As Kreuzberger et al.\ observe, workflow
orchestration tools such as Airflow, Kubeflow, and AWS SageMaker are optimised
for linear production pipelines~\cite{kreuzberger2023mlops}. They lack
deterministic sub-task invalidation for nested experimental loops such as LOSO
cross-validation, where 128 subjects $\times$ 18 configurations
(see Table~\ref{tab:exp_grid} in Chapter~\ref{chap:methodology}) create
2{,}304 interdependent but individually cacheable training operations.
Their cost management principles focus on periodic retraining schedules rather
than state-aware retraining, where computation is skipped entirely when the
underlying configuration has not changed. The framework developed in this
thesis fills this gap at the experimental stage by providing a pre-computation
check that prevents redundant training before it begins.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{MLflow:} Provides experiment tracking, model registry, and
      artifact logging~\cite{zaharia2018mlflow}. While pipeline caching is
      available through extension modules, standard MLflow workflows are
      not documented to encode fold assignments in cache keys, requiring
      custom implementation for LOSO scenarios.

\item \textbf{DVC:} Uses MD5 hashes of pipeline inputs and code to enable
      pipeline stage reuse~\cite{dvc_pipelines}. DVC's key covers code and
      data files but carries no fold-identity component. A cache hit therefore
      cannot distinguish a model trained on subjects 1--127 from one trained on
      subjects 2--128 --- the silent collision this thesis prevents by embedding
      the held-out subject identifier directly in the SHA-256 fingerprint.

\item \textbf{ZenML:} Implements step-level SHA-256 caching with automatic
      invalidation when inputs or code change. However, ZenML does not natively
      encode held-out subject identifiers: users must implement custom
      materializers to parameterise fold assignments, and the resulting keys
      provide no integrity guarantee that training and evaluation sets are
      correctly separated across cache hits.

\item \textbf{Neptune AI and Weights \& Biases:} These platforms excel at
      metric visualisation and experiment logging~\cite{wandb2024docs}, but
      function as observation layers rather than computational caches.
      Proprietary, cloud-hosted metadata stores introduce vendor dependency
      that can threaten long-term experiment reproducibility: the
      discontinuation of Neptune.ai services in 2026~\cite{neptune2026shutdown}
      illustrates that experiment records held in third-party platforms may
      become inaccessible when the provider exits the market. This motivates
      the independent, self-hosted framework proposed in this thesis, where
      all cached artefacts reside on researcher-controlled storage.
\end{itemize}

The limitations identified across academic work and commercial platforms converge
on a single unaddressed gap, which the following section characterises formally.

\section{Research Gap Analysis}
\label{sec:related_gap}
% ============================================================================
The academic work and commercial tools reviewed above share a structural limitation: none provides a validated, documented pattern for fold-aware caching in LOSO cross-validation workflows. Process-level frameworks such as CRISP-ML(Q) prescribe \textit{what} reproducibility requires but do not enforce \textit{how} it is achieved technically~\cite{studer2021crispmlq}. The framework developed in this thesis closes this gap by encoding fold identity cryptographically, making correct cache reuse a structural property rather than a convention.

The core gap is the lack of validated, documented patterns for
implementing fold-aware caching in cross-validation workflows. While tools
like DVC and ZenML provide general pipeline caching infrastructure, adapting
them for LOSO requires custom logic that:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Encodes the held-out subject identifier in cache keys to prevent
      cross-fold contamination
\item Manages hierarchical dependencies between preprocessing, feature
      extraction, and model training stages
\item Validates cache hits against both parameter configurations and
      fold assignments
\end{enumerate}

These requirements are not addressed in standard documentation, and incorrect
implementation risks subtle bugs where models trained on Subject~A are
erroneously reused for evaluating Subject~B.

This thesis provides a reference implementation demonstrating these patterns
in a real LOSO scenario (128-fold cross-validation on the BOAS dataset), with
explicit measurement of cache performance (hit rates, time savings) and
validation of reproducibility. The contribution is not a new MLOps platform,
but a validated approach for extending existing fingerprinting
techniques to cross-validation contexts.

\section{Sleep Stage Classification Methods}
\label{sec:related_sleep}
% ============================================================================

Sleep stage classification is a well-studied problem in biomedical signal
processing, with methods broadly grouped into feature-based and deep learning
approaches.

\subsection{Feature-Based Approaches}

Feature-based sleep staging extracts domain-informed descriptors from EEG
signals---time-domain statistics, frequency-domain band powers, and signal
complexity measures---and feeds them to classical ML
classifiers. Satapathy et al.\ (2024) demonstrate that multi-modality feature
sets with ensemble classifiers achieve competitive performance, particularly
in cross-subject settings where training data per subject is
limited~\cite{satapathy2024multimodal}.

This thesis follows the feature-based paradigm: 149 handcrafted features per
30-second epoch across 6 EEG channels, classified using XGBoost and Random
Forest. For reference, Bakian Dogaheh and Moradi (2023) report 81.3\%
accuracy and Kappa 0.74 using XGBoost on 5-class AASM staging from a
comparable EEG feature set~\cite{bakian2023xgboost}, providing a benchmark
against which the 85.5\% LOSO accuracy achieved in this thesis can be
situated. Feature-based models have the additional advantage of compact
serialized representations well-suited to fingerprint-based caching.

\subsection{Deep Learning Approaches}

Deep learning methods learn representations directly from raw EEG signals.
Perslev et al.\ (2021) present U-Sleep, achieving robust cross-hardware
staging~\cite{perslev2021usleep}. Siddiqui et al.\ (2025) confirm that deep
models require substantially more computational resources than traditional
ensembles while ensemble classifiers remain highly competitive on
standard benchmark datasets~\cite{siddiqui2025}. Deep learning workflows
address the reuse problem through \textit{checkpointing}---saving intermediate
training state to resume interrupted optimisation---rather than fold-level
memoisation. Checkpointing targets a different inefficiency and does not
prevent redundant retraining when only configuration parameters change.
This thesis addresses the complementary gap: fold-level memoisation for
feature-based models, where serialised representations range from
approximately 1--2~MB per fold (XGBoost) to 120--180~MB per fold
(Random Forest), and per-fold storage is tractable on a single workstation.

\subsection{LOSO Evaluation in EEG Research}

LOSO cross-validation is preferred in EEG research because EEG signals
contain strong subject-specific signatures that cause standard $k$-fold
evaluation to overestimate generalisation performance on unseen
subjects~\cite{kunjan2021loso}. LOSO ensures the model is evaluated on
subjects it has never seen, providing a conservative estimate of
generalisation ability. The computational cost scales directly with subject
pool size: 128 subjects $\times$ 18 configurations yields 2{,}304 training
operations, making caching essential for practical parameter search.

(Sections~\ref{sec:related_academic}--\ref{sec:related_gap}) and the
sleep staging domain context (Section~\ref{sec:related_sleep}), the
following section examines how both converge in industrial deployment,
where reproducibility and configuration management become critical
production requirements.

\section{Research-to-Production Gap in ML}
\label{sec:related_industrial}
% ============================================================================

The transition from research prototype to production deployment is a
well-documented challenge in machine learning, independent of the application
domain. Kreuzberger et al.\ observe that many data science projects remain
``manual to a great extent,'' functioning as experimental proofs of concept
that fail to operationalise efficiently~\cite{kreuzberger2023mlops}. The
barriers are consistently identified as reproducibility failures and
undisciplined experiment management rather than algorithmic limitations.

\textbf{Research-to-Production Gap:}
Moving any ML model from prototype to a system that can be reliably
re-evaluated, versioned, and maintained requires systematic experiment
management: the ability to reproduce any prior result on demand, resume
interrupted experiments, and compare model versions without retraining from
scratch. These requirements apply whether the target application is clinical
sleep analysis, speech recognition, or medical imaging---any domain where
iterative model development occurs. Fingerprint-based caching directly
provides this infrastructure: each cached artefact is permanently linked to
the exact configuration that produced it, enabling on-demand result
reproduction without retraining.

\textbf{Configuration Debt and Technical Reproducibility:}
The systems consequences of undisciplined experiment management are
well-documented. Sculley et al.\ (2015) identify \textit{hidden technical
debt} in production ML systems, specifically \textit{configuration debt},
where undocumented parameter interactions and stale pipeline configurations
accumulate silently and degrade reproducibility over
time~\cite{sculley2015hidden}. Fingerprint-based caching structurally
prevents this class of debt: because every cached artefact is keyed by a
cryptographic hash of its complete configuration, it is impossible to serve
a stale result from a superseded parameter set. Every stored model carries
an unambiguous audit trail back to the exact configuration that produced it.
